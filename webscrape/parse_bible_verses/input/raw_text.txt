First Day: Read the Lesson 20 Notes.
The notes and lecture fortify the truth of the passage for understanding and application to daily life.
    1. What truth from the lecture helped you better understand what living for God looks like?

    2. How did the notes challenge you regarding something in your life that feels empty or mechanical? 

Second Day: Read Zechariah 9.
Zechariah 9:1-8 accurately foretells the destructive campaign of Alexander the Great of Greece, which spared Israel but decimated the surrounding territory of Palestine.
    3. a.	From 9:1-8, how did God reveal His grace as He foretold the fate of Israel and its neighbors?

b.	How has God shown you grace when walking through a troubling time?

c.	Read Psalm 91. What truth about God in this psalm encourages you?

    4. a.	List truths about Jesus from verse 9 and tell how each relates to you.

b.	Verse 9 points to Jesus’s first coming, and verse 10 foretells His return. What critical differences do you see in His two advents?

    5. Which verses or phrases from 9:11-17 encourage you to trust God as your protector and deliverer?

Third Day: Read Zechariah 10.
The Lord promised to care for His people.
    6. What promises and warnings are given in verses 1-3?

    7. a.	What descriptive phrases from verse 4 point to Christ, and what does each phrase reveal about 
		Him? (Use any other Scriptures that add to your understanding, such as Isaiah 22:23-24; Daniel 
		2:34; and Romans 13:1.)

b.	List several promises God made to His people (Judah and Ephraim) in verses 5-12.

    8. a. 	Circle each time “I” is used in verses 5-12. What lessons do you learn about God’s relationship with 
		His people and how He orders their circumstances?

b.	How is God strengthening you to stand for Him or rejoice despite difficulty?

Fourth and Fifth Days: Read Zechariah 11.
Zechariah 11 prophesies God’s judgment against Israel because they rejected the Good Shepherd. The first three verses describe the desolation of Israel by the Romans following AD 70. In verses 4-14, God tells Zechariah to portray the Good Shepherd, Jesus, and foretells Israel’s rejection of Christ. The last three verses refer to Israel’s domination by Roman and other rulers but also point to the devastation caused by the Antichrist.
    9. How does the poetry in verses 1-3 reveal trouble awaiting the Israelites?

    10. How does God refer to Israel in verse 4? 

    11. a.	From verse 5, what accusations did God make against Israel’s false shepherds?

b.	From verse 6, how would God respond to the “flock”—the people of Israel? Why? (See also Jeremiah 2:13; Zechariah 11:8; Matthew 16:21; and John 10:1-19.)

    12. From verses 7-9, describe how God led Zechariah to portray himself as the shepherd of God’s people.

    13. From verses 10-11 and 14, what did the breaking of the two staffs, Favor and Union, signify?

    14. a.	From verses 12-13, what did Israel believe the Good Shepherd was worth? (See also Exodus 21:32 
		and Matthew 26:14-16.)

b.	When have you been tempted to detest God, undervalue the Good Shepherd, or exchange Christ for worldly gain?

    15. a.	Verses 15-16 refer to the Antichrist, who will rule with cruelty during the earth’s last days. What do 
		these verses reveal about his power, cruelty, and destruction? 

b.	What do you learn about God from His response to this foolish, powerful leader in verse 17?

Sixth Day: Review Zechariah 9–11.
Jesus Christ, the rejected Shepherd, will reign and rule eternally as conquering King.
    16. How did this lesson call you to trust God, even while living in a world that opposes Him?
